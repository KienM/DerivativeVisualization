Visually Intuiting the Derivative

In this lesson, we look to gain intuition into the concept of the derivative and what it means to take a derivative.

First, what is the derivative? It is the rate of change for a function. Visually, it is the slope of the line tangent to the curve - a rate of output with respect to its input.

Second, why do derivatives matter? It matters, because in the world, things sprout and shrivel and change, and the rate at which this happens matters. You or someone else will be interested in how quickly or sometimes how slowly something happens. Rates of change are important.

Then how do we find the tangent line? Recall that a tangent line touches something at only one point and that a secant line touches at two points. Let's visualize it.

Consider the graph of y = x²:

Recall the definition of slope: a change in y over a change in x.

The light green lines are all secant lines, and the blue is the tangent line. Notice that as the secant lines approach the value of the tangent line, the difference in y over difference in x decreases. For instance, look at the leftmost light line: the difference is fairly large. In contrast, look at the rightmost light line: the difference isn't nearly as large -- and its slope approaches that of the tangent line.

However, these secant lines approximate the tangent; they are not exact values of the tangent line. And they get pretty close to exact -- but not quite. For the exact value, the difference in y over difference in x is infinitely small, and it is denoted by dy/dx. Finding dy/dx involves taking a limit, which is the definition typically presented for the derivative. That is not, however, the point of this lesson; it is to have visual intuition behind what each value of the derivative means, and that is the slope of the line on any function.

In addition, it is important to know that where the function is continuous, a tangent line exists, and therefore, the derivative exists. Moreover, it is important to know that the derivative the function that finds the slopes of these tangent lines and that it is not each line; rather, it is the slope of the line.

Looking back, this shows visually what the line whose slope is found and how that line is found.
